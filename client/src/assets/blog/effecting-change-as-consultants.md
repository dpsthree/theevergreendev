These are the techniques I apply when working with a client on contentious decision points — specifically, when challenging a decision they've already made, whose implementation is underway or complete. The advice is drawn from my experience in software and system architecture, but applies universally to anyone in a consultative role. It's partially inspired by *The Trusted Advisor*.

The format is a series of reflection points to take before your next move, each paired with example questions or actions for gathering information and preparing the team. These steps typically play out over months — occasionally days, occasionally years, depending on the relationship and the scale of the challenge.

---

**1. Be sure it's worth it. Bail out early.**

- Are you in a position to effect change at this level?
- Is your client contact in a position to effect change at this level?
- Is your advice truly an upgrade, or is it a preference or net-neutral trade-off?
- Is it suitable for **this** client?
- Is it worth risking the relationship? The client?
- If you have to think about any of these for more than a second, bring in another consultant for a second opinion.

**2. Know the key stakeholders.**

- Who stands to gain or lose from challenging the existing decision?
- *"Who led the effort to implement X?"*
- *"Who was the original champion of this idea?"*

**3. Understand the organizational and industry scope of the original decision.**

Know who else played a role, and who else would be affected by challenging it.

- *"Where did the original advice come from?"*
- *"How did your team hear about X?"*
- *"Which article was that again? Who wrote it?"*
- *"What are other teams doing to solve this problem?"*

**4. Internalize what this decision means to the stakeholder.**

- *"Did it take a lot of time to implement?"*
- *"Is it fully implemented?"*
- *"How much did it cost?"*
- *"Would you have done anything differently?"*
- *"Would you do it again?"*
- *"Is this something you're willing to revisit?"*

**5. Internalize what this decision means to the rest of the team.**

- *"Did the team support the effort right away, or did they take some convincing?"*
- *"How do they feel about it today?"*

**6. Understand the problem the client is actually trying to solve.**

- *"What is it about X that appeals to you?"*
- *"Have you seen the expected benefit?"*
- *"How has this worked in practice?"*

**7. Assume client competency first. Your initial advice is not always right.**

- *"What other techniques have you considered? Tried?"*
- *"What didn't you like about those?"*

**8. Reevaluate your position. The client knows what's best for the client.**

- *"Would being able to do X be of benefit?"*
- *"Even if it took Y hours to implement?"*

**9. Lead clients to discovery. Don't dictate improvements — buy-in is not optional.**

- *"I noticed this part of the solution seems to be struggling with X. What ways have you thought of to improve it? Have you considered Y?"*
- *"I've built out a prototype. Can we take a look at it together? I think you might have ideas for how to apply it to your project."*
- *"Give me a call the next time you run into that — let's see if we can come up with a solution together."*

**10. Don't be pushy. Work your recommendation into their priorities; don't replace them.**

- *"You mentioned it would be nice to be able to do X. I propose we take that on as part of effort Y."*
- *"We don't have to take this on now, but let's watch for opportunities to switch over."*
- *"Perhaps we can get the benefits you mentioned by taking small steps as we go."*

**11. Be willing to let it go.**

There's more than one path to success, and your metric of success is probably not the client's.

- *"I believe X would give us [important metric improvement]. But I'm happy to keep moving forward with Y. Let me know if the priority changes and we can revisit."*

**12. Don't let client dysfunction cause a breach of contract.**

It's possible for client management and decision-making to be so poor that it creates unacceptable risk to a consultant or their firm.

- Seek advice from your own management and fellow consultants.
- Let your management cancel the engagement if needed.

If points 1–10 are handled well, it should be exceedingly rare that point 12 is needed.

---

## Closing thought

If there's a thesis buried in this list, it's that the hardest part of being a trusted advisor is accepting that you might be wrong — and that the client's definition of success is the one that counts. Points 1, 7, and 11 are really the whole job; everything else is scaffolding for the humility those three require.
